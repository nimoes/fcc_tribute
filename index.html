Tribute to Louis Armstrong

Father of Jazz

I am embarrassed to say that I never listened to Louis Armstrong until my sophomore year, when my college roommate played La vie en rose during our study in Jamestown Hall. I am not a huge music person, and furthermore, I am definitely not a big jazz person either. To my shallow understanding, jazz involves several clashing of several instruments or sounds, and because of its complex, dynamic nature, I always shied away from it and listened to something more...predictable and tunes that I could follow along. But something about Armstrong's La vie en rose (which means "Life in Rosy Hues") was different. Rather, it was a good introduction for someone like me to listen to, and start giving more appreciation towards jazz.

La vie en rose was one of Armstrong's hit music in 1950s. It was orginially written by a French singer, Edith Piaf, in 1945. Although many of his songs were not originally his, adroit musical talents allowed him to tune and tweak each melody and call it his own. Some interesting facts about Louis Armstrong:

  • Born in 1901 in New Orleans, LA, Armstrong first learned how to play cornet.
  • He joined several bands before moving into Chicago, where he played with King Oliver and his Creole Jazz Band.
  • His first recording single dates back to April 5, 1923, when he recorded "Chimes Blues" with Oliver.
  • Although Armstrong was criticized for his race, for his comments on civil rights, personal life issues, he left a strong legacy as an African American jazz musician that we still remember today.
